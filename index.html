Trulli Caroli since 1889, a legacy of stone and wood

Built in 1889, Trulli Caroli is an authentic expression of the rural architecture of the Itria Valley. The trulli, with their dry stone domes, were agricultural shelters or peasant homes, designed to offer natural insulation and functionality with minimal resources.

Next to the trulli, the cummersa represented an evolution in housing: larger, rectangular structures originally built with double-pitched roofs and exposed wooden trusses. Over time, many were internally transformed with stone barrel vaults that replaced the original wooden roof. The cummersa of Trulli Caroli preserves this original structure, renovated with respect and care to highlight the origins of this architecture. A project carried out economically, with great family sacrifice, to preserve the authentic soul of the place.

The Story of Trulli Caroli

My name is Cosimo Caroli, and I am the current owner of Trulli Caroli. It all started with my great-grandfather Count Giuseppeantonio, son of Domenico, born in 1886. At the age of 72, he had his last daughter, Countess Lucia, and when she was just two years old, he passed away at 74. Back then, men had more patience. He began building the trulli and the cummersa as a young man and completed the work in 1889.

The trulli were used as a palmento (grape pressing room) and for other agricultural purposes, while the cummersa served as the dwelling. These structures were typically built near dry-stone walls, right on the rocky spots where nothing could grow, and made as small as possible to avoid wasting fertile soilbecause land meant food and livelihood.

Today, things have changed. Agriculture has declined, ancient trades have nearly disappeared, and maybe the only way to prevent rural depopulation is tourism. Thats why I believed in restoring what I inherited from my grandparents, Lucia and Rocco Caroli. The land had been worked for decades as a vineyard, nearly one hectare, and later became a cherry orchard with over 300 trees.

Now the land lies emptywe need to rethink agriculture with crops that preserve the panoramic view of the Itria Valley, an essential element for visitors, as it conveys peace, nature, and harmony.

In 2008, we started the restoration of the trulli, beginning with the chiancarelle (limestone roof tiles), all done economically within the familythanks to my father Giacomo Caroli and my uncle Tommaso, from whom I learned many trades in construction. Every stone has been preserved in its original place, respecting the authenticity of this centuries-old building tradition. All the limestone was quarried from our land by my great-grandfather.

Working stone is no easy taskespecially with dry-stone construction. It requires skill, patience, and mastery of tools like the masons hammer. In the cummersa, with its wooden trusses, farmers used to hang tomatoes and store food supplies, hidden away from insects and heat.

The restoration took 15 years. A long journey of care, family effort, and personal labor. We poured our hearts into it. United by a dream, we transformed Trulli Caroli into one of the most beloved stays in the Itria Valley.
